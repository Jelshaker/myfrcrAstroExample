    Component Format Duration Focus/Description
    MCQ Section 100 Questions (Single Best Answer) 2 Hours Breadth of knowledge, pattern recognition, fundamental mechanisms, and clinicopathological correlations across the curriculum.
    SAQ Section Typically 10 Questions 1 Hour Depth of understanding, structured synthesis, pathophysiology, etiology, and clinical/radiological correlation.

    Example Pathology MCQ Question

    Question: A 5-year-old child presents with an enlarging, firm, painless abdominal mass. An abdominal ultrasound shows a large, solid, heterogeneous intra-abdominal mass arising from the right kidney, displacing the surrounding bowel. Which of the following statements regarding the most likely diagnosis is MOST accurate?

    A. The tumor peak age of presentation is 15–20 years.
    B. It is frequently associated with a germline mutation in the WT1 gene.
    C. It typically presents with significant hematuria and severe hypertension.
    D. The most common histological subtype consists of a triphasic pattern of blastemal, stromal, and epithelial cells.
    E. It is rarely associated with congenital malformations such as hemihypertrophy or aniridia.

    Correct Answer: D

    Explanation: This describes a Wilms tumor. It typically presents in children aged 2-5, is known for a classic triphasic histological pattern, and has established associations with syndromes like WAGR and Beckwith-Wiedemann.

    Example of pathology Short answer question

    A 65-year-old patient presents with progressive lower back pain. Imaging reveals a lytic, expansile lesion in the vertebral body. A biopsy is performed.

    Question:

    1. List three likely differential diagnoses based on the imaging appearance. (3 marks)
    2. Describe the histological features that would distinguish a Giant Cell Tumor (GCT) from an Aneurysmal Bone Cyst (ABC). (2 marks)
    3. Briefly explain the role of RANK/RANKL signaling in the pathogenesis of this lesion. (1 mark)

    Model Answer / Marking Rubric

    • Part 1 (Differential): Metastasis, Multiple Myeloma, Giant Cell Tumor, Aneurysmal Bone Cyst (Any 3).
    • Part 2 (Histology):
      • GCT: Presence of numerous uniformly distributed osteoclast-like giant cells within a background of mononuclear neoplastic stromal cells.
      • ABC: Blood-filled spaces lined by fibrous septa, typically lacking a neoplastic stromal component; reactive woven bone may be present.
    • Part 3 (Pathogenesis): Overexpression of RANKL by neoplastic stromal cells leads to excessive recruitment and activation of osteoclasts, resulting in focal bone resorption.

    Example multiple response question:

    A 55-year-old male presents with chronic obstructive symptoms and recurrent infections. A CT scan of the chest demonstrates extensive bronchiectasis predominantly affecting the upper lobes, associated with hyperinflation. What is the most likely pathological underlying cause?

    • Cystic Fibrosis (CFTR mutation)
    • Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA)
    • Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency
    • Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia
    • Tuberculosis sequelae

    Correct Answer: C

    Reasoning: Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency characteristically presents with panacinar emphysema that is predominantly basilar; however, when it involves the airways, it can be associated with bronchiectasis. Note for Exam Prep: While ABPA causes central bronchiectasis, the clinical history and specific pattern of distribution are key to differentiating these in an MCQ context. Always look for the "classic" associations in the stem.
